Point of View
What most HR teams get wrong
01
Activities over problems
Surveys, town halls, and policies only matter when they solve a real, named problem. Too often they become ends in themselves, disconnected from what people actually need.
02
Initiatives over trust
People rarely leave because there weren't enough programs. They leave when there's a lack of trust, growth, feedback, or clarity. No initiative fixes a broken feedback culture.
03
Happiness over clarity
Culture is not about making employees happy. It's about creating clarity, trust, growth, and alignment toward a common goal. Happiness follows — it isn't the target.
04
HR as a separate function
The best HR work happens when you understand the business deeply — its revenue drivers, bottlenecks, and the language its leaders speak. Without that, you're just a support function.

A Problem I'm Proud of Solving
Recognition that actually meant something
Recognition was happening — but inconsistently. People were putting in real effort with no shared signal for what good looked like.

At Zocket, rapid growth meant the company was adding people faster than culture could catch up. Recognition existed, but it was ad hoc — reinforcing nothing in particular.

Rather than introducing awards for the sake of it, I worked with leaders to build a structured R&R framework where monthly achievements fed into quarterly recognition at town halls. The goal wasn't just to reward people — it was to create shared clarity around what good performance looked like, and celebrate it publicly.

The framework improved recognition morale by 50%+. More importantly, it gave leaders a language for celebrating their teams — and gave people a reason to stay.

What It Created
01
A shared language for what "good" looked like across teams
02
Peer-led recognition that felt genuine, not performative
03
50%+ improvement in morale on recognition
04
Leaders invested in celebrating their teams publicly
People don't stay because of compensation or perks. They stay when they feel valued, seen, and connected to something bigger than themselves.
